# Partnership proposal: Codex Adoption Lab

## Executive summary

Codex Adoption Lab proposes a bounded, measurable pilot to help QA, product, and analysis professionals move from occasional AI use to repeatable, evidence-backed Codex workflows.

The proposal is based on a simple observation:

> Codex capability is expanding rapidly, while many potential users still need help selecting a first valuable workflow, supervising it safely, measuring the outcome, and connecting proven value to the right subscription or API path.

The Lab would design and operate an eight-week adoption experiment, deliver reusable workflow assets, and report both successful and unsuccessful outcomes. Any performance compensation would apply only to a contractually defined cohort and verified incremental value.

## Why this fits the OpenAI Partner Network direction

OpenAI describes its Partner Network as an ecosystem for organizations that help customers:

- identify valuable AI use cases;
- redesign workflows;
- integrate technology into operating environments;
- drive adoption and change management;
- produce measurable outcomes.

The proposed pilot focuses specifically on workflow selection, activation, evidence, and adoption for an underserved professional segment.

Participation in or acceptance by the Partner Network is not assumed. This repository is an independent evidence package intended to support an application or conversation.

## Proposed engagement

### Name

**Codex Adoption Pilot for QA and Product Workflows**

### Duration

Eight weeks, followed by a thirty-day retained-use measurement window.

### Target cohort

10–20 qualified participants in the initial independent pilot. A sponsored pilot could use a larger cohort only after the seed workflow and instrumentation are validated.

### Services

1. Audience and workflow research.
2. Segment-specific onboarding and education.
3. QA/Product workflow kit design.
4. Seven-day guided Build Week.
5. Acceptance and safety framework.
6. Plan and API decision support.
7. Activation, retention, and value measurement.
8. Failure-mode analysis.
9. Final evidence and expansion recommendation.

### Deliverables

- market and pricing audit;
- launch strategy;
- participant screener;
- first-workflow selector;
- context-pack and task-decomposition templates;
- human approval map;
- acceptance rubric;
- outcome receipt;
- anonymized case studies;
- metric dictionary and dashboard specification;
- final evidence report;
- recommendation for a second cohort or stop decision.

## Proposed commercial models

The exact commercial model would need to comply with OpenAI procurement, partner, privacy, brand, and attribution requirements.

### Option A — fixed-fee pilot

A fixed fee covers research, design, facilitation, measurement, and reporting.

**Best when:** reliable revenue attribution is unavailable.

**Benefit:** simple administration and no pressure to push participants toward an unsuitable paid plan.

### Option B — fixed fee plus performance component

A fixed implementation fee covers delivery, with an additional success fee for agreed outcomes.

Possible success measures:

- qualified participant activation;
- first accepted workflow;
- thirty-day repeat use;
- verified team deployment;
- attributable net-new subscription or API revenue.

**Recommended starting structure:** fixed pilot fee plus a capped variable component. Revenue share should be used only when the required billing and cohort data are available.

### Option C — verified incremental revenue share

Illustrative negotiation range:

- **5–10% of verified incremental net revenue**;
- limited to the attributed pilot cohort;
- limited to the first six months after activation;
- subject to a pre-agreed cap;
- calculated after refunds, credits, chargebacks, taxes, and excluded revenue;
- payable only where a defensible baseline and tracking method exist.

This range is a proposal, not an OpenAI program term or market standard.

## Required attribution contract

No performance model should begin before the parties agree on:

### Cohort

- participant ID rules;
- enrollment period;
- geography and segment;
- existing versus new customer status;
- treatment of team expansion;
- duplicate and fraudulent account handling.

### Baseline

- pre-pilot plan and usage;
- prior paid intent or active sales opportunity;
- organic conversion expectation;
- seasonality and concurrent campaigns;
- existing partner or affiliate influence.

### Conversion event

Examples:

- first paid subscription;
- Plus-to-Pro expansion;
- individual-to-Business expansion;
- incremental Business seats;
- first paid API usage above a threshold.

### Revenue definition

```text
Gross attributed revenue
- taxes
- refunds
- chargebacks
- promotional credits
- unrelated product revenue
- pre-existing contracted spend
= eligible net revenue
```

### Attribution window

Recommended initial window: six months from the participant's first accepted workflow, with a separate thirty-day activation and retention report.

### Incrementality method

Preferred methods, in descending order of confidence:

1. randomized holdout;
2. matched comparison cohort;
3. interrupted time-series or historical baseline;
4. last-touch or referral-code attribution with explicit limitations.

Referral-code attribution alone proves association, not necessarily incrementality.

## Brand and trust boundaries

Until written authorization exists, the project must:

- state that it is independent;
- avoid OpenAI logos or partner badges suggesting endorsement;
- avoid claiming access to internal pricing, customer, or roadmap data;
- use only public product information;
- obtain permission before publishing participant identity or results;
- distinguish official OpenAI statements from Lab hypotheses.

## Data and safety requirements

Before any sponsored execution, define:

- allowed data classes;
- prohibited secrets and regulated data;
- storage and retention periods;
- participant consent;
- access controls;
- incident reporting;
- deletion process;
- human approval requirements;
- use of anonymized or synthetic examples.

The initial independent cohort should use non-sensitive or appropriately sanitized work.

## Evidence standard

Every headline result in the final report should include:

- metric definition;
- numerator and denominator;
- sample size;
- source;
- collection method;
- confidence label;
- known bias;
- whether the value is measured, estimated, or self-reported.

Example:

> Among 14 eligible starters, 9 completed a human-reviewed workflow within seven days (64%). This was measured from participant logs. Artifact quality and time savings were not independently audited.

## Decision gates

### Gate 1 — independent seed evidence

Required before a formal performance proposal:

- at least five completed workflows;
- at least three accepted outcomes;
- documented failures and corrections;
- stable measurement definitions;
- no unresolved critical safety issue.

### Gate 2 — sponsored pilot readiness

Required before representing the engagement publicly:

- written scope and brand permission;
- approved participant and data policy;
- attribution contract;
- commercial terms;
- named accountable owners;
- stop conditions.

### Gate 3 — scale decision

Scale only when:

- activation and repeat use are credible;
- facilitator effort is sustainable;
- the workflow creates measurable value;
- the cohort shows a reasonable commercial path;
- the experiment does not rely on misleading urgency or plan pressure.

## Draft outreach message

> We have created an independent Codex Adoption Lab focused on helping QA, product, and analysis professionals move from occasional AI use to completed, evidence-backed workflows. Rather than proposing a broad marketing engagement, we are designing a bounded pilot with explicit safety boundaries, activation and retention metrics, cost-per-workflow measurement, and transparent failure reporting. We would like to explore whether this could fit the OpenAI Partner Network, a partner-led adoption program, or a jointly defined experiment. We are open to a fixed pilot structure and, where attribution is technically and contractually possible, a capped performance component tied only to verified incremental outcomes.

A 30-minute qualification discussion covering:

1. whether this segment and adoption problem are strategically relevant;
2. whether a small independent evidence package would be useful;
3. which partner or product team would own evaluation;
4. what customer, data, brand, and attribution standards would apply;
